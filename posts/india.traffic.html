Thats the best illustration I can think to characterize how cars, pedestrians, buses, motorcycles, cows and dogs move around the Indian streets. Everyone predicts the other persons/animals movement and adjusts their own to avoid collision at all costs. I want to see those AI engineers trying to built a car that can navigate this good! ### video https://photos.app.goo.gl/miPKy75Rwk16PTw18 ### paragraph And this leads to my next point: honking. We tried to play a came and count the seconds in between honks heard. I have to tell you it was impossible to count milliseconds! Although I do not know this first hand, I believe that honking here is used as a form of navigation around vehicles. ### paragraph It's kind of like a communication signal. Not like how we are used to to say “hey, look out I am right behind you” but rather, “car is coming from the other side”, “I am going to go around you now”, “don’t change direction”, “change direction”, “stop”, “go” and so on. Oh, and of course some of the honks are there to just say a friendly “hello”. Its a new language invented here where duration, frequency, intensity and volume of honking counts. ### photo https://photos.app.goo.gl/g4nsTfXGwCo3AVJJ6 City traffic ### paragraph We recently experienced a traffic jam that occurred because two buses wanted to go on opposite directions on a street clearly not built to support that.
